The Ultimate Shortcut

You have suffered through the long, boring algebra of First Principles. You have earned the right to learn the mathematician's greatest time-saver.

First Principles is crucial for understanding what a derivative is. But in the real world, it is simply too slow.

Imagine your exam asks you to find the derivative of this monster function:

The Nightmare

$$f(x) = 7x^3 + 5x^2 - 9$$

If you used the limit formula, you would have to expand $(x+h)^3$, then $(x+h)^2$, multiply them all out, carefully cancel the terms, and eventually take the limit. It would take a full page of working out, and one single minus-sign error would ruin the whole thing.

Luckily, mathematicians spotted a pattern. We call it The Power Rule.

The Official Power Rule

"Multiply by the power, then subtract one from the power."

$$f(x) = ax^n \implies f'(x) = anx^{n-1}$$

Applying the Power Rule

Worked Solutions

🟢 Easy: A Basic Polynomial

Question: Differentiate $f(x) = 5x^3$

1. Bring the power (3) to the front and multiply it by the coefficient (5): $5 \times 3 = 15$

2. Subtract 1 from the power: $3 - 1 = 2$

$$f'(x) = 15x^2$$

🟡 Medium: Multiple Terms

Question: Differentiate $f(x) = 4x^4 - 2x^2 + 7x - 9$

Apply the rule to each piece individually. Remember that $7x$ is $7x^1$, and a constant number like $-9$ has a slope of $0$!

$$f'(x) = (4 \times 4)x^3 - (2 \times 2)x^1 + (7 \times 1)x^0 - 0$$ $$f'(x) = 16x^3 - 4x + 7$$

🔴 Hard: Negative Indices

Question: Differentiate $f(x) = \frac{3}{x^2}$

1. Trap Warning! You cannot use the power rule while $x$ is on the bottom. Rewrite it using negative indices first.

$$f(x) = 3x^{-2}$$

2. Now apply the rule. Multiply by $-2$, and subtract 1 from the power (be careful: $-2 - 1 = -3$).

$$f'(x) = -6x^{-3}$$

3. (Optional) Rewrite with positive indices:

$$f'(x) = -\frac{6}{x^3}$$
