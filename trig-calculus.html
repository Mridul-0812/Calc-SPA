Trigonometric Calculus

The maths of oscillations, waves, and circles. Discover how the rates of change of trig functions create a beautiful, never-ending cycle.

1. The Primary Trig Functions ($\sin$, $\cos$, $\tan$)

The Core Three Derivatives:
$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x$
$\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x$
$\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x$

Before we memorise the rules, let's prove where they come from. Deriving these functions relies heavily on foundational limits and trigonometric identities.

Deriving $\sin(x)$ from First Principles

We use the angle addition formula $\sin(A+B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$ and the standard limit $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h} = 1$.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin(x)}{h}$$ $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin x \cos h + \cos x \sin h - \sin x}{h}$$ $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \left[ \sin x \left( \frac{\cos h - 1}{h} \right) + \cos x \left( \frac{\sin h}{h} \right) \right]$$ $$f'(x) = \sin x \cdot (0) + \cos x \cdot (1) = \cos x$$

Deriving $\tan(x)$ using the Quotient Rule

Since $\tan(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}$, we can simply apply the quotient rule: $\frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{\sin x}{\cos x}\right] = \frac{(\cos x)(\cos x) - (\sin x)(-\sin x)}{(\cos x)^2}$$ $$= \frac{\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x}$$ $$= \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} = \sec^2 x$$

2. The Reciprocal Functions ($\sec$, $\operatorname{cosec}$, $\cot$)

The reciprocal functions often intimidate students, but they are easily derived using the quotient or chain rule. By rewriting them with negative indices, differentiation becomes straightforward.

Deriving Secant: $\frac{d}{dx}[(\cos x)^{-1}] = -1(\cos x)^{-2}(-\sin x) = \frac{\sin x}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{1}{\cos x} \cdot \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} = \sec x \tan x$

Deriving Cosecant: $\frac{d}{dx}[(\sin x)^{-1}] = -1(\sin x)^{-2}(\cos x) = -\frac{\cos x}{\sin^2 x} = -\frac{1}{\sin x} \cdot \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} = -\cosec x \cot x$
$\frac{d}{dx}[\sec x] = \sec x \tan x$

$\frac{d}{dx}[\cosec x] = -\cosec x \cot x$

$\frac{d}{dx}[\cot x] = -\cosec^2 x$

3. Worked Examples

🟢 Easy: Trig Chain Rule

Find $y'$ for $y = \sin(4x^2)$

  • Inside: $u = 4x^2 \implies u' = 8x$
  • Outside: $\sin(u) \implies \cos(u)$
  • Multiply: $y' = 8x \cdot \cos(4x^2)$

🟡 Medium: Product Rule

Find $y'$ for $y = x^3 \tan(x)$

  • $u = x^3 \implies u' = 3x^2$
  • $v = \tan(x) \implies v' = \sec^2(x)$
  • $y' = u'v + uv'$
  • $y' = 3x^2 \tan(x) + x^3 \sec^2(x)$

🔴 Hard: Nested Reciprocals

Find $y'$ for $y = \cosec^3(2x)$

  • Rewrite: $y = [\cosec(2x)]^3$
  • Power rule: $3[\cosec(2x)]^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\cosec(2x)]$
  • Inner deriv: $-\cosec(2x)\cot(2x) \cdot 2$
  • Combine: $-6\cosec^3(2x)\cot(2x)$
