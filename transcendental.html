Transcendental Functions

Algebra limits what we can model. To understand continuous growth, decay, and the geometry of nature, we need to step into the calculus of $e^x$ and $\ln(x)$.

The Most Important Number in Math: $e \approx 2.71828...$
It is the unique base where the rate of growth of the function is exactly equal to the value of the function itself.

2. The Exponential Chain Rule

When we have $e^{f(x)}$, the derivative is simply the derivative of the exponent multiplied by the original function: $f'(x)e^{f(x)}$.

Worked Examples: Exponential

🟢 Easy: Standard Chain

Differentiate $y = e^{4x^3}$

  • Inside function $f(x) = 4x^3$
  • Derivative $f'(x) = 12x^2$
  • Multiply to front: $y' = 12x^2 \cdot e^{4x^3}$

🔴 Hard: Product Rule Hybrid

Differentiate $y = x^3 e^{-2x}$

  • $u = x^3 \implies u' = 3x^2$
  • $v = e^{-2x} \implies v' = -2e^{-2x}$
  • $y' = 3x^2 e^{-2x} - 2x^3 e^{-2x}$
  • Factor: $y' = x^2 e^{-2x}(3 - 2x)$

3. The Natural Logarithm $\ln(x)$

The derivative of $\ln(x)$ is simply $\frac{1}{x}$. If there is a function inside, like $\ln(f(x))$, the derivative is $\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$.

Worked Examples: Natural Logs

🟢 Easy: Composite Log

Differentiate $y = \ln(x^4 + 1)$

  • Rule: $\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$
  • Inside $f(x) = x^4 + 1$
  • Deriv $f'(x) = 4x^3$
  • Result: $y' = \frac{4x^3}{x^4 + 1}$

🔴 Hard: Log Laws First!

Differentiate $y = \ln(\sqrt{3x+2})$

  • Rewrite root: $y = \ln((3x+2)^{1/2})$
  • Bring down power: $y = \frac{1}{2}\ln(3x+2)$
  • Differentiate: $y' = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{3}{3x+2}$
  • Result: $y' = \frac{3}{6x+4}$
