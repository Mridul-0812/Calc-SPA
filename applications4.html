Applications: Motion & Kinematics

Welcome to physics! By tracking a particle's position over time using $x(t)$, we can use derivatives to instantly uncover its velocity and acceleration. Calculus is the mathematical engine of motion.

The Mathematics of Movement

In standard Grade 11 mechanics, we assume a particle is moving along a straight line (1-Dimensional motion). Time is represented by $t$ (where $t \ge 0$).

The Core Trio & Notation:
  • Position/Displacement: $x(t)$
    Where the particle is relative to the origin. '$t$' represents time and '$x$' represents displacement.
  • Velocity: $v(t) = x'(t) = \dot{x}(t)$
    The rate of change of position. How fast and in what direction it's moving. Differentiate $x(t)$ once. (Newtonian dot notation $\dot{x}(t)$ is heavily used here!)
  • Acceleration: $a(t) = v'(t) = x''(t) = \ddot{x}(t)$
    The rate of change of velocity. Is it speeding up or slowing down? Differentiate $x(t)$ twice.

Key Phrase Translation: "Initially" means $t = 0$. "At rest" means $v(t) = 0$. "Stationary point" means $v(t) = 0$. "Constant velocity" means $a(t) = 0$.

Worked Solutions

🟢 Easy: Basic Motion

A particle moves according to $x(t) = t^2 - 4t$. Find its initial position and its velocity at $t = 3$.

  • Initial Position: "Initial" means $t = 0$. Substitute into $x(t)$.
    $x(0) = (0)^2 - 4(0) = 0$ m.
  • Find Velocity Function: Differentiate $x(t)$ to find $v(t)$.
    $v(t) = \dot{x}(t) = 2t - 4$.
  • Substitute $t = 3$: $v(3) = 2(3) - 4 = 6 - 4 = 2$.
  • Result: Velocity is $2$ m/s (moving in the positive direction).

🟡 Medium: Finding "At Rest"

If $x(t) = 2t^3 - 9t^2 + 12t$, find the times when the particle is at rest, and its acceleration at those times.

  • Find $v(t)$: $v(t) = 6t^2 - 18t + 12$.
  • Set $v(t) = 0$: "At rest" means velocity is zero.
    $6t^2 - 18t + 12 = 0 \Rightarrow 6(t^2 - 3t + 2) = 0$
    Factorising: $6(t-1)(t-2) = 0$.
    The particle is at rest at $t = 1$ and $t = 2$.
  • Find $a(t)$: Differentiate velocity. $a(t) = \ddot{x}(t) = 12t - 18$.
  • Substitute Times:
    At $t=1$: $a(1) = 12(1) - 18 = -6$ m/s$^2$.
    At $t=2$: $a(2) = 12(2) - 18 = 6$ m/s$^2$.

🔴 Hard: Trig Kinematics

A particle's displacement is given by $x(t) = 3\sin(2t)$. Find its acceleration when it is first at rest ($t > 0$).

  • Find $v(t)$: Apply the Chain Rule. $v(t) = 6\cos(2t)$.
  • Find First Time at Rest: Set $6\cos(2t) = 0$.
    $\cos(2t) = 0$. The first positive solution is $2t = \frac{\pi}{2} \Rightarrow t = \frac{\pi}{4}$.
  • Find $a(t)$: Differentiate velocity. $a(t) = -12\sin(2t)$.
  • Calculate Acceleration: Substitute $t = \frac{\pi}{4}$.
    $a(\frac{\pi}{4}) = -12\sin(2 \times \frac{\pi}{4}) = -12\sin(\frac{\pi}{2})$.
    Since $\sin(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 1$, the acceleration is $-12$.

Mastery Practise

1. Easy: A particle has displacement $x(t) = 5t^2 + 2t$. What is its velocity at $t = 1$?

2. Easy-Medium: Given $x(t) = e^{3t}$, what is the initial acceleration of the particle? (i.e. $\ddot{x}(t)$ at $t=0$)

3. Medium: A particle's position is $x(t) = t^3 - 6t^2$. At what time $t > 0$ is the particle momentarily at rest?

4. Hard: Let $x(t) = 4\cos(t) + t$. What is the initial velocity of the particle?

5. Legend: A particle's displacement involves the Product Rule: $x(t) = t e^{-t}$. At what exact time $t$ does the acceleration equal zero?
