The Magic of "Right Now"

To master calculus, you must forget everything you know about average speed over time. We are here to calculate exactly what is happening in a single, frozen moment.

Imagine tracking a race car. Finding its average speed over a 10-second window is easy—just measure the distance it traveled and divide by 10. That's basic geometry.

But what if you want to know the car's speed at exactly second 4.000? You need the time gap to be zero. But you can't divide by zero; math breaks. So, how do we cheat the system? We use Limits to shrink the gap infinitely.

But mathematicians hate relying on graphs to find answers. We use algebra. Here is the master formula that calculates exactly what happens when the gap ($h$) becomes zero:

The Master Limit Formula

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

Interactive 3: Beating the "Divide by Zero" Boss

Let's prove algebraically that the derivative of $f(x) = x^2$ is indeed $2x$. Click to perform the algebra.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h}$$

We substituted $x^2$ into the formula. We can't make $h=0$ yet, or we divide by zero!

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) - x^2}{h}$$

We expanded the perfect square brackets.

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h}$$

The $x^2$ and $-x^2$ perfectly canceled each other out.

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h)$$

We factored out $h$ from the top and bottom. The "divide by zero" trap is destroyed!

$$f'(x) = 2x$$

Now that it's safe, we let $h$ become zero. The algebra perfectly matches our graphs!

Finding Derivatives via First Principles

Worked Solutions

Let's put the master formula to work. Here is how you strictly calculate the derivative formula $f'(x)$ for different types of functions, purely using algebra.

🟢 Easy: A Linear Function

Question: Find the derivative of $f(x) = 3x + 2$ from first principles.

1. Substitute $f(x+h)$ and $f(x)$ into the master formula:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(3(x+h) + 2) - (3x + 2)}{h}$$

2. Expand the brackets carefully:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3x + 3h + 2 - 3x - 2}{h}$$

3. Cancel matching terms ($3x$ with $-3x$, and $2$ with $-2$):

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3h}{h}$$

4. Divide by $h$ to finish:

$$f'(x) = 3$$

🟡 Medium: A Quadratic with a Coefficient

Question: Find the derivative of $f(x) = 5x^2$ from first principles.

1. Substitute into the formula (remember the $5$ stays outside the square!):

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{5(x+h)^2 - 5x^2}{h}$$

2. Expand $(x+h)^2$ first, then multiply by 5:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{5(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) - 5x^2}{h}$$ $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{5x^2 + 10xh + 5h^2 - 5x^2}{h}$$

3. Cancel the $5x^2$ terms, then factor out $h$:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h(10x + 5h)}{h}$$

4. Cancel $h$ and let the remaining $h \to 0$:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} (10x + 5h) = 10x + 0 = 10x$$

🔴 Hard: Multiple Terms & Cubics

Question: Find the derivative of $f(x) = x^3 - 4x$ from first principles.

Beginner's Guide to Multiple Terms: The biggest mistake students make is substituting incorrectly. When evaluating $f(x+h)$, you must replace every single $x$ in the equation with $(x+h)$ in brackets. For $x^3 - 4x$, it becomes $(x+h)^3 - 4(x+h)$. Do not just slap an "$+h$" at the end! Also, note that $(x+h)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3$.

1. Substitute safely into the limit definition, using large brackets for the second part:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{((x+h)^3 - 4(x+h)) - (x^3 - 4x)}{h}$$

2. Expand absolutely everything:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3 - 4x - 4h) - x^3 + 4x}{h}$$

3. The magic step: every term without an $h$ must cancel out. ($x^3$ cancels $-x^3$, and $-4x$ cancels $+4x$):

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3 - 4h}{h}$$

4. Factor out $h$ from the top:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h(3x^2 + 3xh + h^2 - 4)}{h}$$

5. Cancel the $h$ fractions, then let the remaining $h$s become zero:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} (3x^2 + 3xh + h^2 - 4) = 3x^2 - 4$$
