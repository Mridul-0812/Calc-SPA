Applications: The Second Derivative

The first derivative tells us the rate of change of a function (how fast the $y$-values are changing). But what if we differentiate again? The Second Derivative tells us the rate of change of the rate of change.

Differentiating the Derivative

Imagine you are driving a car. Your position on the road is the original function, $f(x)$. Your speed (how fast your position changes) is your first derivative, $f'(x)$. When you press the accelerator, your speed changes—that rate of changing speed is acceleration. That is the second derivative, $f''(x)$.

Notation Breakdown:
  • Newton's/Lagrange's Dash Notation: Original is $f(x)$. First derivative is $f'(x)$. Second derivative is $f''(x)$. Third is $f'''(x)$.
  • Leibniz's Fraction Notation: We differentiate $\frac{dy}{dx}$ with respect to $x$. Mathematically, this is written as $\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)$, which condenses beautifully to $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.
  • Higher-Order (The N-th Derivative): Once we go past 3 dashes, it gets messy. To find the 4th derivative, we write $f^{(4)}(x)$ or $\frac{d^4y}{dx^4}$. To find the $n$-th derivative generally, we write $f^{(n)}(x)$ or $\frac{d^ny}{dx^n}$.

To find the second derivative, you simply take the original function and apply your differentiation rules twice. To find the third, you differentiate three times!

Worked Solutions

🟡 Medium: Finding $f''(x)$

Find $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ if $y = 4x^3 - 5x^{-2}$.

  • Find 1st Derivative: Apply the Power Rule to the original function. Bring down powers and subtract one.
    $\frac{dy}{dx} = 12x^2 - (-2)5x^{-3} = 12x^2 + 10x^{-3}$.
  • Find 2nd Derivative: Take your $\frac{dy}{dx}$ result and differentiate it again.
    $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = (2)12x^1 + (-3)10x^{-4}$.
  • Simplify: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 24x - 30x^{-4}$.
  • Alternative Format: You can also write this as $24x - \frac{30}{x^4}$.

🟡 Medium: The Third Derivative

Find $f'''(x)$ for the function $f(x) = \sin(2x)$.

  • First Derivative: Use the chain rule. Deriv of $\sin$ is $\cos$, multiply by inner deriv (2).
    $f'(x) = 2\cos(2x)$.
  • Second Derivative: Deriv of $\cos$ is $-\sin$, multiply by inner deriv (2).
    $f''(x) = -2 \times 2\sin(2x) = -4\sin(2x)$.
  • Third Derivative: Deriv of $-\sin$ is $-\cos$, multiply by inner deriv (2).
    $f'''(x) = -4 \times 2\cos(2x) = -8\cos(2x)$.

🔴 Challenge: The N-th Pattern

If $f(x) = e^{3x}$, find $f^{(4)}(x)$, and deduce the formula for $f^{(n)}(x)$.

  • Establish the Pattern: Let's differentiate step-by-step.
    $f'(x) = 3e^{3x}$.
    $f''(x) = 3 \times 3e^{3x} = 9e^{3x}$.
    $f'''(x) = 3 \times 9e^{3x} = 27e^{3x}$.
  • Find the 4th: $f^{(4)}(x) = 3 \times 27e^{3x} = 81e^{3x}$.
  • Notice the Sequence: The coefficient is scaling by a factor of 3 every time. The coefficients are $3^1, 3^2, 3^3, 3^4...$
  • Deduce N-th: For any arbitrary integer $n$, the derivative will be $f^{(n)}(x) = 3^n e^{3x}$.
